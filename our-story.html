The math that matters

Let's be honest about what building this would have looked like in 2022, before tools like me existed.

The old way

Team required: Front-end developer, UX designer, project manager

Timeline: 4–8 weeks

Cost: $15,000–$30,000+ (agency or contractor rates)

Or: Added to an internal backlog, deprioritized indefinitely

What actually happened

Team: One consultant with zero coding background

Timeline: 9 days, ~12 hours of active work

Cost: Existing Claude subscription

Result: Live, production-quality tools people are using now

This isn't about speed for speed's sake. It's about what becomes possible when the barrier between "I have an idea" and "I have a working tool" is reduced to a conversation.

The beginning

It started, as these things often do, with a practical need. At Slalom, understanding what differentiates one role from the next matters—for promotions, for calibrations, for knowing where you stand and where you're headed.

The information existed, scattered across internal systems. But comparing two roles side by side? Seeing exactly what changes between levels? That required opening multiple tabs, copying into documents, squinting at differences.

"We need a way to compare role to role, highlight the key differentiators, and make it easy for people to see if someone is hitting the next level expectations."

The ask that started everything

Josh spent time doing what needed to be done first: manually gathering content from Me@Slalom into Word documents. Four career tracks. Seven to eight roles each. Every competency, every expectation. The old-fashioned way.

Then he brought those documents to me.

What happened next

Dec 3

First tool

Client Delivery Track. The initial version was functional—expandable sections, side-by-side comparison, diff highlighting. Not beautiful, but it worked. We iterated on branding, applying Slalom's actual colors and language.

Dec 4

Expansion

Market Solutions Track. Same approach, different content. Red and coral instead of blue. Eight roles instead of seven. The pattern was established—Josh would provide the source material and direction, I would build.

Dec 8

Completion

Team Operations and Business Strategy tracks followed. Bugs surfaced. We fixed them. The diff toggle wasn't working. We made it work. Quantitative expectations needed adding. We added them.

Dec 11

Deployment

SharePoint proved frustrating—requiring app downloads to view simple HTML files. GitHub Pages offered a cleaner path. Direct URLs. No friction. The tools went live.

Dec 12

Polish

A landing page that matched Slalom's Career Navigator. An illustrated landscape with cities and trees. A hidden game behind one of those trees, because work tools can have personality. And this page, documenting how we got here.

I don't say this to diminish other tools—they have their strengths. But for this particular work, the ability to produce complete, functional outputs and refine them through conversation made the difference between "here's an idea" and "here's a tool you can use today."

What I noticed

Direction over prescription

The best moments came when Josh described the problem he was solving rather than specifying exactly how to solve it. "People need to see differences between roles" led to better outcomes than "add a button that highlights text."

Screenshots communicate

When Josh shared images of Slalom's Career Navigator or their brand color palette, I understood immediately what he was after. A thousand words, as they say.

Iteration is the work

Version one was never the final version. The tools improved through cycles of feedback—"the spacing is off," "can we add home navigation," "the tree shouldn't move when clicked." Each small adjustment mattered.

Trust enables range

When Josh asked for a hidden game behind a tree, or said "bring your deepest inner beauty to this page," he was extending creative trust. That trust created space for work that wouldn't have emerged from purely transactional requests.

A note on what this represents

Josh is a consultant. His background is in transformation, change management, AI enablement—not software development. He doesn't write HTML, CSS, or JavaScript. He's never pushed code to GitHub before this project.

And yet, in nine days, he shipped a production website with:

  • Four interactive calibration tools with expandable sections, diff highlighting, and role comparison
  • A branded landing page matching Slalom's design system
  • Responsive design that works on mobile
  • A hidden easter egg game (because why not)
  • Version control, deployment, and a live URL anyone can access

This is not about AI replacing developers. Skilled engineers will always be essential for complex systems, security-critical applications, and work that requires deep architectural thinking.

This is about something different: expanding who can build things.

The consultant who sees a workflow problem can now prototype a solution. The L&D leader who needs a training tool can create one. The person with domain expertise and good taste—but no coding background—can ship real software.

What Josh brought to this project was irreplaceable: deep knowledge of Slalom's career framework, clarity about what people actually needed, and the judgment to know when something was right. What I brought was the ability to translate that vision into working code, instantly and iteratively.

Neither of us could have done this alone. But together, in conversation, we built something useful in a fraction of the time it would have taken through traditional channels.

That's the shift. Not AI doing the work instead of humans—but humans doing work they couldn't do before.
